\subsection{Families of Subsets}

\begin{remark*}[Exposition]
Volume I develops sets, membership, subsets, unions, intersections,
complements, and power sets as primitive set-theoretic tools.  This chapter
uses those tools at the next level.  Instead of asking only whether a point
belongs to a set, it asks which subsets of a fixed set are being selected and
which operations keep the selected subsets inside the same collection.
\end{remark*}

\begin{definition}[Family of Subsets]\label{def:family-of-subsets}
Let \(X\) be a set.  A \emph{family of subsets of \(X\)} is a collection
\(\mathcal{F}\) such that
\[
  \mathcal{F} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X).
\]
Equivalently, every member of \(\mathcal{F}\) is itself a subset of \(X\).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark*}[Standard quantified statement]
\[
  \mathcal{F}\text{ is a family of subsets of }X
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \forall A\,(A\in\mathcal{F}\Rightarrow A\subseteq X).
\]
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[Interpretation]
There are two membership levels.  The relation \(x\in A\) says that a point
belongs to a subset \(A\) of \(X\).  The relation \(A\in\mathcal{F}\) says
that the subset \(A\) has been admitted into the family \(\mathcal{F}\).
Algebras of sets study the second level.
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[Examples]
If \(X=\mathbb{R}\), the collection of all bounded intervals is a family of
subsets of \(X\).  The collection of all finite subsets of \(X\) is also a
family of subsets of \(X\).  The full power set \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) is the
largest possible family of subsets of \(X\).
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[Non-Examples]
A collection that contains an object which is not a subset of \(X\) is not a
family of subsets of \(X\).  For instance, if \(X=\mathbb{R}\), then
\(\{\{0\},\mathbb{R},\mathbb{C}\}\) is not a family of subsets of \(X\),
because \(\mathbb{C}\nsubseteq\mathbb{R}\).
\end{remark*}
